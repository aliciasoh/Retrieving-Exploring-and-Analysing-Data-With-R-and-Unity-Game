Social Media Data




Using the online web tools, we conducted a quantitative analysis to understand the effectiveness of the Mozzie Wipe Out Campaign.

From the social media graphs above, we can see that NEA mostly includes links and photos across all social media platforms. Links received the most interaction rates, with high shares, likes and comments, while photos and videos have a lower rate. We can see that the social media platform aims to capture the target audience’s attention through visuals and graphics due to their short attention span. Notably, videos have an exceptionally high amount of shares, and hence allowing for NEA to reach out to more social networks. Since videos take up a relatively small proportion of their types of posts, it would be recommended for NEA to focus on videos as a means of raising awareness and perhaps fostering more action on prevention of mosquito breeding.

The timing of posts are largely at 6am in the morning, which might be slightly too early as people would likely not be scrolling through social media at that timing and actively commenting on social posts. Even if they might be scrolling through the posts, they might not have the ability to take in the information and process it as they might still be sleepy and not as clear-headed as compared to in the afternoon. Further, it was reported that the best times to post on social media is in fact 9am, 1pm and 3pm (Ellering, 2016). As such, we recommend NEA to post at these timings in order to receive a higher engagement rate with their followers.

Generally, the number of interactions from users remain low - a clear indicator that the social media campaign is not enough.

Additionally despite the ongoing initiative on social media, the dengue rates continued to rise. We can see this in the correlation between the social media metrics and the dengue rates graph. Despite the spike in social media data in October 2015, dengue rates still spiked in November 2015. In conclusion, there is little correlation between NEA’s social media initiative and incidence rates of dengue.

Conclusion: Social media data did not suggest that there is any correlation between social media usage and incidence rates of dengue
